    Reactions

    Supported reactions are currently limited to Unit 1 and Unit 2 of MIT 5.12.

    Hydrogenation

    H2 cat. Pd/C in ethanol

    Regiochemistry: None

    Stereochemistry: Syn addition

    Alkenes and alkynes are candidates for this reaction. Pd|C refers to palladium on carbon, a catalyst used in hydrogenation reactions. This reaction adds hydrogens to the alkene or alkyne, converting it all the way to a single bond.

    Read more: palladium on carbon, hydrogenation


    H2, Pd|C, EtOH

    Hydrohalogenation

    HX in CH2Cl2 (either 1 equiv. or excess)

    Regiochemistry: X added to more substituted carbon

    Stereochemistry: None (neither syn nor anti)

    Alkenes and alkynes are candidates for this reaction. This reaction adds a halogen and a hydrogen to an alkene (or double that, in case of an alkyne), with the halogen added to the carbon specified by Markovnikov's rule. Since the reaction proceeds through a carbocation intermediate, there is no stereochemistry.

    Note that this reaction is most efficient when carried out in the dark -- allowing light will cause trace amounts of free-radical hydrohalogenation, which is anti-Markovnikov.

    Useful note: It is possible to specify that you want to add only one equivalent of HX to the reactant. For alkynes, this results in addition only once.

    Read more: hydrohalogenation, carbocation


    HBr in CH2Cl2

    Halogenation

    X2 in CH2Cl2

    Regiochemistry: None

    Stereochemistry: Anti addition

    Alkenes and alkynes are candidates for this reaction. This reaction adds two halogens (or double that, in case of an alkyne). Since the reaction proceeds through a halonium intermediate, the two halogens are added anti.

    Useful note: It is possible to specify that you want to add only one equivalent of X2 to the reactant. For alkynes, this results in addition only once.

    Read more: halogenation, halonium


    Br2 in CH2Cl2

    Free-Radical Hydrohalogenation

    HBr cat. peroxide, with heat or light

    Regiochemistry: Br added to less substituted carbon

    Stereochemistry: None (neither syn nor anti)

    Alkenes are candidates for this reaction. Free-radicals flip the regiochemistry of the usual hydrohalogenation reaction. Peroxide favors the formation of a free-radical bromine atom, which reacts with the alkene. The intermediate has a carbon radical which is most stable as the more substituted carbon. The result is anti-Markovnikov addition.

    Read more: hydrohalogenation, peroxides


    HBr, ROOR, light

    Epoxidation

    mCPBA or RCO3H, in CH2Cl2

    Regiochemistry: None

    Stereochemistry: Can be thought of as syn addition

    Alkenes are candidates for this reaction. mCPBA is a peroxycarboxylic acid, and catalyzes the conversion of double bonds to epoxides. Two stereochemical outcomes are possible, depending on which side of the alkene the oxygen adds to. The cis or trans orientation of the alkene is preserved.

    Read more: epoxidation, mCPBA


    mCPBA in CH2Cl2

    Tert-butoxide Elimination

    KOC(CH3)3

    Alkyl halides are candidates for this reaction. Removes a halide and its antiperiplanar hydrogen, creating a new double bond. This is an E2 reaction.

    Note: Any other substitution and elimination reactions (covered in Unit 3 of 5.12) are not implemented.

    Read more: tert-butoxide, E2 elimination reactions


    Tert-butoxide in DMSO

    Hydration of Alkenes

    H2SO4 in H2O; or H2SO4 in ROH

    Regiochemistry: Oxygen is attached to the more substituted carbon

    Stereochemistry: None (neither syn nor anti)

    Alkenes are candidates for this reaction. When H2O is present, the alkene is converted to an alcohol. Addition of an H+ creates a carbocation intermediate, and then -OH is added to the most substituted carbon. When an alcohol, ROH, is added, an ether is produced instead. The reaction is neither syn nor anti addition, since it involves a carbocation intermediate, and follows Markovnikov's rule.

    Read more: acid-catalyzed hydration


    H2SO4 in H2O
    H2SO4 in ethanol

    Hydration of Alkynes

    H2SO4 in H2O, HgSO4 accels; or H2SO4 in ROH, HgSO4 accels

    Regiochemistry: Oxygen is attached to the more substituted carbon

    Stereochemistry: None (neither syn nor anti)

    Alkynes are candidates for this reaction. When H2O is present, one pi bond of the alkyne is broken, forming an enol. Addition of an H+ creates a carbocation intermediate, and then OH is added to the most substituted carbon. This produces an enol, which will then tautomerize to form a carbonyl. When an alcohol, ROH, is added, an ether is produced instead. The reaction is neither syn nor anti addition, since it involves a carbocation intermediate, and follows Markovnikov's rule.

    Read more: acid-catalyzed hydrationreactions of alkynes


    H2SO4 in H2O, HgSO4 accels.

    Halohydration

    X2 in H2O, or X2 in ROH

    Regiochemistry: Oxygen is attached to the more substituted carbon

    Stereochemistry: Anti addition

    Alkenes are candidates for this reaction. The halogen attaches to the alkene first, followed by a hydroxyl (-OH) or ether (-OR) group, breaking a double bond. The reaction proceeds via a halonium intermediate, and is therefore an example of anti addition. When ROH is used instead of H2O, an ether is formed. Markovnikov's rule applies: the halogen is added to the less substituted carbon.

    Read more: halohydration, halonium


    Br2 in H2O

    Hydroboration-Oxidation

    Regiochemistry: -OH is attached to less substituted carbon

    Stereochemistry: Syn addition

    Hydroboration

    BH3 in THF

    Alkenes and alkynes are candidates for this reaction. Borane adds to one carbon of the alkene, transferring its hydrogen to the other carbon while breaking a pi bond. The reaction is anti-Markovnikov: the borane attaches to the less substituted carbon.

    Oxidation

    NaOH and H2O2

    Alkenes and alkynes are candidates for this reaction. Peroxide attacks the boron atom, ultimately replacing -BH2 with -OH. The final result is an anti-Markovnikov hydroxyl group. If the original reactant was an alkyne, it is possible that the resulting enol will tautomerize to form a carbonyl.

    Read more: hydroboration-oxidation, borane, THF


    BH3 in THF
    NaOH and H2O2

    Dihydroxylation

    cat. OsO4, NMO, acetone or H2O

    Regiochemistry: None

    Stereochemistry: Syn addition

    Alkenes are candidates for this reaction. Catalytic amounts of OsO4 in the presence of NMO yields syn addition of two hydroxyl groups to a double bond.

    Read more: dihydroxylation, NMO, OsO4


    cat. OsO4, NMO, in acetone

    Ozonolysis

    O3 with Me2S or Zn, in CH2Cl2

    Regiochemistry: None

    Stereochemistry: None

    Alkenes are candidates for this reaction. After a complicated reaction mechanism involving all three of the oxygens of ozone, double bonds are split in two, yielding two new carbonyls.

    Read more: ozonolysis


    O3 with Me2S, in CH2Cl2

    Lindlar Reduction

    H2 cat. Lindlar

    Regiochemistry: None

    Stereochemistry: cis

    Alkynes are candidates for this reaction. Catalyzed by the Lindlar catalyst, hydrogen adds to triple bonds, resulting in cis double bonds.

    Compare to: Sodium-Ammonia Reduction

    Read more: Lindlar catalyst, alkyne reductions


    H2 cat. Lindlar

    Sodium-Ammonia Reduction

    Na, in NH3 (L)

    Regiochemistry: None

    Stereochemistry: trans

    Alkynes are candidates for this reaction. Catalyzed by a solution of sodium in liquid ammonia, hydrogen adds to triple bonds, resulting in trans double bonds.

    Compare to: Lindlar Reduction

    Read more: alkyne reductions


    Na, in NH3 (L)

    Forming an Acetylide

    NaNH2 in NH3

    Regiochemistry:

    Stereochemistry:

    Alkynes which end in hydrogens are candidates for this reaction. The pKa of ammonia, NH3, is 34; the pKa of acetylene, HCCH, is 25. The amide ion, NH2-, is highly basic and able to deprotonate acetylene and other molecules containing triple bonds with terminal hydrogens. Adding sodium amide in ammonia deprotonates acetylene, producing acetylide.

    Read more: acetylide


    NaNH2 in NH3

    Acetylide Addition

    (no reagents needed)

    Regiochemistry: None

    Stereochemistry: None

    Acetylides, once formed, can be used to form carbon-carbon bonds. The nucleophilic acetylide displaces a leaving group such as chloride or iodide in a substitution reaction, if the leaving group is attached to a primary alkyl group. A molecule of the form R-CH2-Br will suffice.

    Read more: acetylide


    Tautomerization

    (no reagents needed)

    Not so much a reaction, as more of an automatic process. Hydroxyl groups attached to double bonds (enols) will tautomerize, resulting in a ketone or an aldehyde.

    Read more: enol tautomerization, tautomers
